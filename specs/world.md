# Ghost Driver — World

## Obsidian Bay
*"A city that swallowed the future whole and never looked back."*

Obsidian Bay is a retro-futuristic noir metropolis built on the edge of a dark coastal harbor. The city's skyline is a collision of Art Deco grandeur and advanced technology — ornate stone facades strung with neon signage, elevated rail lines cutting between cathedral spires, holographic wanted posters flickering on rain-slicked walls. The harbor below is black and glassy, reflecting everything above it like a dark mirror.

The city runs on ambition, corruption, and secrets. The law is well-funded and well-armed. The underworld is older than the law and knows every street better. Somewhere between them, the Ghost Driver vanishes.

---

## Setting & Era
Obsidian Bay exists in an alternate past — roughly equivalent to the late 1920s in culture, fashion, and architecture, but with technology that never existed. The world never chose between the elegance of that era and the advancement of later decades. It took both.

**What stayed 1920s:**
- Art Deco architecture — ornate facades, geometric patterns, grand archways
- Fashion and culture — fedoras, long coats, jazz clubs, speakeasies
- Social structure — organized crime, corrupt officials, powerful syndicates
- Vehicle aesthetics — running boards, rounded fenders, chrome trim, spoke wheels

**What advanced beyond:**
- Turbocharged and electric drivetrains in period-styled bodywork
- Holographic displays embedded in period signage
- Advanced communications — police dispatch technology, electronic surveillance
- Elevated transport infrastructure — rail lines, cable systems
- Experimental weaponry and gadgetry available to those who can afford it

**What this creates:**
A city that feels timeless. Not the past, not the future — its own era entirely. Players should feel like they've arrived somewhere that couldn't exist anywhere else.

## The City Map

### Map Philosophy
Obsidian Bay is designed around the chase. Every district, every street, every alley serves the gameplay first and atmosphere second. Good map design for Ghost Driver means:

- **Multiple viable routes** between any two points — no single optimal path
- **Chokepoints** that create tension — bridges, tunnels, narrow gates
- **Escape valves** — alleys, garages, shortcuts that reward map knowledge
- **Readable hierarchy** — arterial roads, secondary streets, back alleys immediately distinguishable at top-down view
- **District identity** — each area looks and feels different from the others

### Map Size
Single city map for the full campaign. Large enough to feel like a real city with distinct districts. Small enough that players develop genuine map knowledge over the course of the campaign. The city should feel mastered by Act 3 — players know where every parking garage is, every useful alley, every cop chokepoint.

## City Districts

### 1. The Financial District
*"Where the money lives and the law never sleeps."*

The beating heart of Obsidian Bay. Tall Art Deco towers, grand bank facades, wide arterial boulevards. Heavy civilian traffic during the day. The most cop-dense district in the city.

**Gameplay characteristics:**
- Wide roads — high speed pursuits, easy to get boxed in
- Limited alley access — escape relies on speed and gadgets over shortcuts
- Multiple parking structures — garages are the primary ditch tool here
- Roadblocks pre-positioned here at high pursuit levels
- Mission 1 (First Run) begins here

**Visual identity:** Amber streetlights on wide boulevards. Marble facades. Geometric tile patterns on the street. Holographic bank signage. Rain-glazed cobblestones.

---

### 2. The Docks
*"Everything that enters Obsidian Bay passes through here first."*

A working waterfront district. Warehouses, cargo cranes, shipping containers, narrow loading lanes. The smell of salt and engine oil. The Syndicate's primary operational territory.

**Gameplay characteristics:**
- Narrow lanes between warehouses — tight pursuit, easy to lose cops in the maze
- Large open loading areas — cops can surround you if caught in the open
- Water on one edge — dead end if you hit the harbor
- Shipping containers as destructible obstacles
- Tunnels under the dock infrastructure — break helicopter line of sight
- Multiple missions end or begin here

**Visual identity:** Industrial neon on dark warehouses. Crane silhouettes against the night sky. Wet cobblestone loading bays. Ship lights on the black harbor water.

---

### 3. The Backstreets
*"The part of the city the maps don't show."*

A dense residential and small business district. Narrow streets, tight corners, low buildings. The most complex navigation area in the city. Rewarding to master, punishing to enter unprepared.

**Gameplay characteristics:**
- Narrowest streets in the city — cops in large vehicles struggle here
- Highest alley density — the best district for losing pursuit on foot of the map
- Market stalls and street vendors — destructible obstacles everywhere
- Low lighting — Kill Lights most effective here
- Grappling hook most useful district — corners everywhere
- Undercover missions favor this district for blend-in potential

**Visual identity:** Laundry lines between buildings. Neon signs in multiple languages. Street vendors under awnings. Puddles reflecting fragments of colored light. Jazz from open windows.

---

### 4. The Heights
*"Old money lives up here. New money dies trying to reach it."*

An elevated residential district built on the hill overlooking the harbor. Wide curved roads, manicured parks, grand private residences. The city's wealthiest neighborhood — and its most surveilled.

**Gameplay characteristics:**
- Elevated roads with long sight lines — helicopter pursuit most effective here
- Limited cover — few garages, few alleys
- Steep gradient roads — speed behaves differently on inclines
- Spectacular views of the city — visually dramatic chase sequences
- Low civilian density at night — easier to go dark with Kill Lights
- Dead ends near cliff edges — dangerous territory to enter without an exit plan

**Visual identity:** Wrought iron gates. Stone walls. Manicured hedgerows. Grand residences with lit windows. The harbor visible far below between buildings.

---

### 5. The Industrial Quarter
*"The city's engine room. Loud, ugly, and indispensable."*

A working industrial zone of factories, rail yards, and processing plants. Wide service roads for heavy vehicles. The armored truck's natural habitat.

**Gameplay characteristics:**
- Wide service roads — built for heavy vehicles, armored trucks excel here
- Rail crossing gates — timing-based obstacles, can trap pursuing cops
- Large open factory yards — dangerous if surrounded, useful for high-speed maneuvers
- Tunnels and underpasses — best helicopter counter-play district
- Destructible industrial equipment — barrels, pallets, loading equipment
- Low civilian density — fewer penalties for aggressive driving

**Visual identity:** Spark showers from factory windows. Steam venting from pipes. Rail lines crossing the road. Massive cylindrical storage tanks. Functional brutalist architecture softened by age.

---

### 6. The Neon Mile
*"Vice district. The law looks the other way — for a price."*

Obsidian Bay's entertainment and vice district. Clubs, speakeasies, theaters, illicit establishments. Bright neon on every surface. The most visually striking district in the city.

**Gameplay characteristics:**
- Dense civilian foot traffic — highest civilian collision risk in the city
- Medium-width roads — not as tight as Backstreets, not as wide as Financial District
- Multiple parking structures — garages accessible
- High ambient noise — police sirens less audible to the player here (audio design)
- Undercover vehicles blend perfectly — everyone is coming and going
- Several mission objectives located here

**Visual identity:** Maximum neon density. Reds, greens, blues, ambers all competing. Marquee signs. Crowds under awnings. Jazz and blues audible from clubs. The most alive district in the city at night.

---

### 7. The Waterfront Promenade
*"Where Obsidian Bay puts on its best face."*

A scenic coastal strip between the Docks and the Financial District. Wide promenade roads, ornamental lighting, the harbor on one side and grand facades on the other. Beautiful and exposed.

**Gameplay characteristics:**
- Long straight promenade road — maximum speed, minimum cover
- Open sight lines — helicopter pursuit devastating here
- Few escape routes — entering the Promenade is a commitment
- Bridges connecting to other districts — chokepoints under heavy surveillance at high pursuit levels
- Dramatic visual backdrop for chase sequences
- The harbor edge is a hard boundary — nowhere to go

**Visual identity:** Ornamental lamp posts. Harbor railings. The black water of Obsidian Bay reflecting the city lights. Grand hotel facades. The most cinematic chase backdrop in the game.

## Key Environmental Features

### Parking Garages
Distributed across all districts except The Heights. Primary ditch tool — cops cannot see inside.
- Each garage has one or two entrances — committing to a garage is a risk
- Some garages have through-routes — enter one side, exit the other
- Level 4+ pursuit sends units to watch garage exits — cannot hide indefinitely at maximum heat

### Tunnels
Located primarily in the Industrial Quarter and under the Docks infrastructure.
- Breaks helicopter line of sight while inside
- Cops still pursue through tunnels — not a hiding spot, a sight-line break
- Some tunnels are one-way or too narrow for armored units

### Alleyways
Densest in the Backstreets, present in most districts.
- Narrow enough to block large cop vehicles
- Grappling hook anchor points on alley corners
- Some dead-end alleys — map knowledge critical

### Bridges
Three major bridges connect districts across the harbor inlet.
- Chokepoints at high pursuit levels — roadblocks pre-positioned here
- Under the bridges: water. Driving off a bridge ends the mission.
- Tactical value: crossing a bridge commits you to the other side

### Rail Crossings
Located in the Industrial Quarter and outer districts.
- Automated crossing gates create timing puzzles
- A cop car caught behind a closed gate is temporarily blocked
- Player caught behind a gate at high pursuit is in serious trouble

### The Elevated Highway
An elevated highway ring circles the outer edge of Obsidian Bay, connecting all major districts via on-ramps and off-ramps. The fastest road surface in the city — and the most dangerous place to be at high pursuit levels.

**Gameplay characteristics:**
- Highest top speeds achievable — sports cars and the Phantom reach full potential here
- Longest sight lines in the game — helicopter pursuit most punishing, no cover whatsoever
- On-ramps and off-ramps are tactical decision points — exit into a district to use its terrain, or stay on the highway and outrun pursuit
- Ramp exits can be blocked by roadblocks at pursuit level 4+ — committing to the highway is a risk
- Elevated position means driving off the edge is a mission-ending fall — guardrails can be destroyed
- At night, the highway offers the most dramatic visual sequences in the game — city spread out below, cop lights in the mirror

**On-Ramp / Off-Ramp Ditch Zones**
Each ramp transition is a potential ditch opportunity:
- Bail onto a ramp at speed — pursuing cops overshoot and have to loop back
- Exit into the Backstreets mid-chase — instantly changes the terrain in the player's favor
- A cop car committed to highway speed struggles to navigate a sudden ramp exit
- Players who know the ramp locations can use them as escape valves from otherwise inescapable highway pursuits

**Construction Zones**
Two active construction zones exist on the highway — sections of the elevated ring that are incomplete. A Ghost Driver signature moment:
- A gap in the highway surface requires a full-speed jump to clear
- Only certain vehicles make it — speed and mass both factor in
  - The Spectre: clears it easily
  - The Razorback: makes it at full speed
  - The Pilgrim / Drifter: barely makes it — requires nitro boost
  - The Ironside / Warden: risky — needs nitro, tight margin
  - The Vault: cannot make the jump — too heavy, too slow
- Pursuing cops must make the same calculation — interceptors may follow, heavy units will not
- Construction debris on approach — barrels, barriers, equipment to navigate before the jump
- The gap is a one-way commitment — no turning back once you've committed to the approach speed
- One construction zone is accessible early campaign, the second unlocks as a shortcut in Act 3

### The Drawbridge (Docks District)
A working drawbridge spanning the harbor inlet in the Docks district. The most iconic single environmental feature in Obsidian Bay.

**How it works:**
- The drawbridge operates on a timed cycle — raises and lowers at set intervals
- Cycle timing is shown on a dockside signal light visible from a distance — player can read it
- A raised drawbridge is an impassable barrier — even the Vault cannot cross it
- A lowering drawbridge is a closing window — cross it in time or abort

**Ditch opportunities:**
- **The classic:** Cross at the last moment as the bridge begins to raise — pursuing cops commit and drive into the harbor below
- **The bait:** Approach at speed, brake at the last second and redirect — cops committed to the crossing go over the edge
- **The timing play:** Read the signal light, plan the approach, arrive exactly as it reaches maximum clearance — pure skill execution
- **The abort:** If timing is wrong, off-ramps on both sides of the bridge allow last-second redirects into the Docks maze

**Bridge raise trigger (late campaign):**
In Act 3, a mission-specific interaction allows the player to trigger the drawbridge manually via a dockside control panel — a one-time use per mission that guarantees the raise but requires stopping briefly to activate. High risk, guaranteed reward.

**Narrative weight:**
The drawbridge appears in Mission 17 (The Syndicate's Play) as a key route to the final delivery location at the docks. Whether it's raised or lowered at that moment is a timed event the player must account for in their approach.

---
- Hard boundary — driving into the harbor ends the mission
- Visually present in multiple districts as atmosphere
- The name-sake of the city — at night, the water is black and glassy

## Day / Night Cycle
Missions are set at specific times of day established in the briefing. No real-time cycle during gameplay — the time of day is a mission design tool.

**Night missions (majority of campaign):**
- Kill Lights mechanic fully effective in dark areas
- Neon and artificial lighting dominates visual palette
- Cops rely more on pursuit patterns than visual recognition
- The Backstreets and Industrial Quarter become much darker

**Day missions (select missions):**
- Kill Lights ineffective — no darkness to exploit
- Civilian traffic heavier
- Cop visual recognition faster
- The Heights and Financial District most used for day missions
- Higher visual contrast — less atmospheric but tactically different

**Dawn / Dusk (specific dramatic missions):**
- Transitional lighting used for narrative weight
- Mission 20 (Vanish) ends at dawn — the Ghost Driver disappears into the light

## Civilian Traffic
Civilians populate the streets of Obsidian Bay and serve several gameplay functions:

- **Navigation obstacles** — traffic to weave through during chases
- **Collision consequence** — too many civilian collisions triggers heat even without cops present
- **Cover during undercover missions** — blending into traffic is the point
- **Environmental storytelling** — the city feels alive

**Civilian behavior:**
- React to the player's speed — pedestrians scatter, civilian cars swerve
- Heavy collisions cause civilian cars to stop and block lanes — can trap both player and cops
- During high pursuit levels, civilian traffic thins — the city clears out instinctively
- Night missions have lighter civilian traffic than day missions

## Weather & Atmosphere
Rain is the default state of Obsidian Bay. Not always heavy — sometimes a mist, sometimes a downpour — but always present or recently past.

**Rain effects:**
- Wet road surfaces — slightly increased slide on handbrake turns
- Puddle splashes on impact
- Neon reflections on wet streets — primary visual identity of the game
- Reduced visibility at high speeds in heavy rain

**Weather variation by mission:**
- Light drizzle — standard, atmospheric
- Heavy rain — reduced visibility, more dramatic
- Fog — rare, used for specific atmospheric missions, reduces sight lines for both player and cops
- Clear night — used sparingly, makes the city feel unusually exposed

**The harbor fog:**
A recurring atmospheric element. Fog rolling in off Obsidian Bay is both visual flavor and occasional gameplay tool — dense harbor fog can obscure the Waterfront Promenade enough to use Kill Lights effectively even at night.

## Obsidian Bay Identity
The city should feel like it has existed for centuries and will exist for centuries more. The Ghost Driver is one story in a city full of stories.

**What makes Obsidian Bay feel real:**
- District names on loading screens and mission briefings
- Civilian dialogue snippets referencing city life
- Wanted posters (holographic) updating with the Ghost Driver's notoriety
- Radio chatter from police referencing specific streets and landmarks
- The harbor always visible from elevated points — the city knows where it is

**What Obsidian Bay is not:**
- A real city — no real-world references
- A generic city — every district has identity
- A backdrop — the city is a participant in the chase
