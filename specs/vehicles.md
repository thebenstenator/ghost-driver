# Ghost Driver — Vehicles

## Philosophy
Every vehicle in Ghost Driver is a tradeoff. Speed vs durability. Gadget capacity vs handling. Anonymity vs power. No vehicle is strictly the best — the right car depends on the job, the notoriety situation, and the player's style.

Each vehicle has a distinct personality that should be felt within the first 30 seconds of driving it.

---

## Vehicle Stats
Every vehicle is defined by six core stats, each rated 1-5:

| Stat | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Speed** | Top speed — how fast it gets going |
| **Acceleration** | How quickly it reaches top speed |
| **Handling** | Turn radius, responsiveness, drift behavior |
| **Durability** | How much damage it can absorb before handling degrades |
| **Mass** | Affects ramming power and resistance to being rammed |
| **Stealth** | How long it takes cops to recognize and respond to this vehicle — affected by notoriety tier |

## Vehicle Classes

### Sedan
The backbone of the Ghost Driver's garage. Unremarkable by design — that's the point.

- Balanced stats across the board
- Best stealth rating of any class — blends into traffic naturally
- Most forgiving to drive — good starting vehicle
- Reliable in undercover missions — looks like any other car on the road
- Moderate gadget slots

**Role:** Everyday workhorse. Low heat management. Mid-mission objective vehicle. Undercover operations.

---

### Sports Car
Fast, aggressive, and impossible to ignore. The weapon of choice when pure speed is the answer.

- Highest speed and acceleration in the roster
- Lowest durability — takes damage quickly
- Twitchy handling at high speed — rewarding to master, punishing to misuse
- Low stealth — cops notice it fast, notoriety escalates quicker
- Fewest gadget slots — built lean

**Role:** High-speed extraction. Time-critical missions. Late campaign escapes. High risk, high reward.

---

### SUV
The thinking player's vehicle. Slower than a sports car but brings tools to the fight.

- Above average durability and mass
- Good gadget slot count — most flexible loadout vehicle
- Handles well for its size — not agile but predictable
- Medium stealth — noticeable but not conspicuous
- Can push through light roadblocks without full stop

**Role:** Long missions with multiple objectives. Players who rely heavily on gadgets. Sustained pursuit endurance.

---

### Armored Truck
A rolling fortress. Slow, loud, and absolutely relentless.

- Highest durability and mass in the roster by far
- Can ram through full roadblocks that stop every other vehicle
- Slowest top speed — cannot outrun interceptors in a straight line
- Lowest handling — wide turns, no drift
- Highest gadget slot count — carries the most equipment
- Zero stealth — immediately suspicious, pursuit starts faster

**Role:** Specific mission requirements. Cash transport jobs. Situations where brute force beats finesse. Players who want to feel unstoppable at the cost of speed.

---

### Work Vehicles (Cover Class)
Not owned by the player — mission-specific cover vehicles used during undercover phases. Cannot be kept in the garage.

Includes:
- **Utility Van** — slow, boxy, completely unremarkable
- **Taxi** — blends into city traffic, slightly faster than van
- **Police Cruiser** — highest risk/reward cover vehicle, instantly suspicious if behavior breaks protocol
- **Delivery Truck** — large, slow, useful for blocking sight lines during swap

**Role:** Undercover missions only. Each has unique behavioral rules for maintaining cover. Cannot use gadgets while in cover vehicle.

---

### Specialty / Exotic
Rare, unique vehicles earned through specific campaign moments. Each has an unusual stat profile that doesn't fit neatly into other classes.

- Acquired through specific missions, not purchasable
- Each one has a backstory tied to the campaign narrative
- Highest visual distinctiveness — stand out in the city
- High notoriety escalation risk — memorable vehicles get remembered

**Role:** Late campaign. Player expression. Reward for campaign completion.

## Garage System

### Garage Screen
- Shows all owned vehicles with front 3/4 and rear 3/4 detailed illustrations
- Displays each vehicle's current notoriety tier
- Access vehicle upgrades, gadget slot purchases, and visual mods from here
- Visual mods change the illustration — repainted car looks different in garage view

### Garage Capacity
- Player starts with 2 garage slots
- Additional slots purchasable with in-game currency
- Maximum garage size: 6 vehicles
- Encourages deliberate fleet management — can't just hoard everything

## Visual Design Notes

### Gameplay Sprites (Top-Down)
- Each vehicle has a distinct silhouette readable at top-down scale
- Color coded by class in default state — players can identify vehicle type at a glance
- Damage states visible: scratches → dents → smoke → heavy damage
- Notoriety visual mods reflected in sprite (repainted sprite after visual mod purchase)

### Garage Illustrations (Front 3/4 and Rear 3/4)
- Higher detail 2D illustrations — where the retro-futuristic noir aesthetic fully expresses itself
- Noir lighting — dramatic shadows, neon reflections on wet bodywork
- Each vehicle has a distinct personality in its design: The Pilgrim looks anonymous, The Razorback looks angry, The Phantom looks dangerous
- Visual mods reflected in illustrations — different paint, plates, body kit variants
- Artist collaboration opportunity — these illustrations are the visual identity of Ghost Driver

### Retro-Futuristic Design Language
All vehicles blend 1920s aesthetic with advanced technology:
- Art Deco body lines and chrome trim
- Advanced turbocharged or electric drivetrains implied by sound design
- Running boards, rounded fenders, spoke wheels — but sleek and modern in proportion
- No contemporary real-world car references — all designs are original to the Ghost Driver world
